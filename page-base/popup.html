about

My research explores the intersection of humour, graphic design, and vernacular art. It is grounded in my personal collection and my design practice which focuses on everyday images and their cultural significance. I am particularly interested in understanding how humour functions in art and design and how everyday imagery contributes to the generation of meaning in visual culture. The two aspects are interrelated: humour is often embedded in daily life and vernacular design, and through visual means they can reveal insights about cultural and artistic values. I start by examining how humour works from philosophical and sociological perspectives, which lead me to discover the cultural context and historical position of vernacular design. Then I investigate the use of humour and vernacular in graphic design through the works of visionary designers. Lastly, I consider humour within the context of both China, my home country, and a broader global perspective. Collectively, these sources help me to establish a critical framework for understanding humour and vernacular in graphic design, as well as the potential of using design to reflect and engage with everyday life.

What memories are held within the rings of a tree? The Atomic Tree is a VR experience into the memories of one of the most revered trees in the world - the 400-year-old Japanese White Pine bonsai that witnessed - and survived - the atomic blast in Hiroshima.
